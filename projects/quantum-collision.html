Quantum collisions

Particles and waves behave inherently differently. In the everyday world, this distinction is clear: particles (think: marbles) travel in straight lines on well defined trajectories, waves (think: water) spread out moving in all directions, going around corners and even through walls.

In the microscopic world, this distinction is less clear. Particles can sometimes behave like waves, and waves can sometimes behave like particles. At the University of Toronto, we cooled a cloud of atoms down to near absolute zero temperature. As we cool, we cross the threhold from the everyday classical world, to the microscopic quantum world. With these atoms we can explore this wave-particle duality and observe matter-wave interference effects.

Such interferences are in fact prominent during the collision of a quantum particle with a barrier. In our experiments we can freeze out the dynamics during a collision and directly probe these rich interference structures which reveals key information on the interactions between the particle and the target.


The technical article was published in Physical Review A, R. Chang et al, PRA 88, 053634, 2013, and is also available on the arXiv: arXiv:1303.1137.
