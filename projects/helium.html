Single-atom counting of ultracold clouds

Over the past twenty years, fhe study of ultracold atoms has come to dominate the field of atomic, molecular and optical physics, with hundreds of experiments around the world. In the vast majority of studies, information regarding the image cloud is inferred from optical images of the atom cloud. Just like in photography, such images integrate over the line-of-sight, and information regarding the depth of the object is often lost.

In the Atom Optics group at l'Institut d'Optique, we have been working with a novel electronic detection system that allows us to reconstruct the complete three-dimensional distribution of the atom cloud at the single atom level, providing us with direct access to many-body quantum correlations.

How it works: Electronic detection

In our experiments, we first prepare a many-body state of ultracold atoms. Such states are typically characterized by the presence of quantum correlations between the atoms in the cloud, which we can directly access with our detector. To detect the cloud, we release the atom cloud, allowing it to freely expand while falling under gravity. During the free-fall, the cloud changes in shape and significantly expands in size due to the distribution of velocities in the initial state. After about 330 milliseconds, the atom cloud arrives at our electronic detector, positioned about 50 cm from the initial cloud position.

For these experiments, we work with Helium atoms in an excited metastable state. Such atoms are special in that they contain a tremendous amount of internal energy (about 20 eV) -- essentially, these atoms are like tiny bombs. When they strike a metal, they "explode", releasing their internal energy and ionizing electrons from the surface of the metal. In our case, we use a device called a Micro-Channel Plate detector (MCP). This plate is perforated by an array of holes (channels). As metastable helium particles fall onto this detector, electrons are ionized, cascading down through the channel ionizing more and more electrons, essentially creating an avalanche-like effect. We have thus amplified a microscopic signal, a single atom, into a macroscopic signal of a few thousand electrons.

This electron shower we then detect using a device called a delay-line anode. This is essentially just a copper wire wound back-and-forth. The electron shower strikes the wire, creating a pair of electrical pulses that counter-propagate along the wire. We detect these pulses, and track their arrival times using a custom FPGA setup which is able to operate at speeds up to 4 MHz.

By looking at the difference in arrival time of these two pulses, t_{x1}-t_{x2}, we can determine the position along the wire that the atom struck. In our case, we have two such delay lines, wound in perpendicular directions, providing information along the transverse directions x and y. Lastly, by looking at the average arrival time of these pulses, we obtain information regarding the vertical direction z.

In the actual experiment, we have tens of thousands of electronic pulses arriving at our detectors, four for each atom event, per experimental run. To correctly identify atom events, we test various combinations of four pulses (quadruplets), one along each channel, to see if they could correspond to a physically meaningful signal. Our reconstruction must also be robust against electronic noise (excess of pulses or loss of pulses). We currently work in a low noise regime where we are able to reconstruct over 90% of atom events.

Quantum atom optics: The Hanbury-Brown and Twiss effect

As seen in Figure 1, our system is able to detect individual atoms from our atom cloud. In many ways, this is equivalent to a single-photon detector (or rather, single-photon camera). Such devices have revolutionized the study of quantum optics. For cold atoms, MCPs have been used to observe the famous Hanbury-Brown and Twiss effect with atoms, directly revealing the quantum statistics of cold bose and fermi gases [M. Schellekens et al. Science 310, 648 (2005), T. Jeltes et al. Nature 445, 402 (2007)].

In the mid 1950s, Hanbury-Brown and Twiss studied the correlation in arrival of photons at two detectors, D_1 and D_2, from a distant star S (see Figure 4). Stars are hot chaotic bodies that spew out light with thermal statistics. It it was thus to the surprise of many that they observed that these photons tended to arrive from the source at the same time -- that is to say the arrival of photons was correlated. This effect, known as photon bunching, can be explained from a quantum mechanical perspective.

Because the star is a hot thermal source, it can be viewed as the sum of independent emitters S_1,S_2,S_3.... At the detectors D_1 and D_2, we detect two photons, say coming from source S_1 and S_2. There are two possible ways that this occurs. In the direct path, the photon from S_1 arrives at D_1 and photon from S_2 arrives at D_2 (S_1\rightarrow D_1, S_2\rightarrow D_2). In the exchage path, we have S_1\rightarrow D_2 and S_2\rightarrow D_1.

From a classical perspective, we can always distinguish these different pathways, direct and exchange, and so we treat them as independent events and sum their probabilities P_{classical}=|A_{direct}|^2 +|A_{exchange}|^2. From a quantum mechanical perspective, these two pathways are indistinguishable and there is an interference. For photons, which are bosonic particles, the amplitudes are summed, P=|A_{direct}+A_{exchange}|^2=2P_{classical}

The Hanbury-Brown and Twiss effect stimulated a better understanding of quantum correlations and is today celebrated as a modern day pillar of quantum optics. Using cold atoms with, we have observed this phenomenon with atoms. We use clouds at a low but finite temperature of a few microKelvin as our thermal source. These atoms are released onto our electronic detector where our single-particle sensitivity allows us to study density-density correlations, as characterized by a joint detection probaility g^{(2)}. We observe the enhancement in the simultaneous detection of two atoms g^{(2)}>1, a manifestation of quantum statistics. It is interesting to contrast this to the case of a Bose-Einstein condensate (BEC). Unlike a thermal cloud, BECs are at absolute zero temperature and thus are represented as a single source S. As such, there is only one possible pathway to the joint detection of atoms (S\rightarrow D_1, S\rightarrow D_2). With only one pathway, there is no interference and no enhancement of detection g^{(2)}=1.
